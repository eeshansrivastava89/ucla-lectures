A prediction is only useful if someone acts on it. A churn score of 0.87 means nothing to a marketing manager who needs to decide what to do about it. This lecture wraps the prediction API with an LLM so raw scores become explanations and suggested actions.

This lecture makes the course different from most MLOps curricula — <span class="accent">you are not just deploying a model, you are building the full product architecture</span> used by teams shipping AI features in production.

But an LLM in production is a new source of risk. It can fabricate facts, ignore instructions, and be manipulated through its input. These are not separate problems — they are instances of the same problem we raised in Lecture 1: <span class="accent">the system will produce wrong outputs, and the design must handle that.</span> Guardrails, grounding in real data via RAG, and fallback to the model's predicted values when the LLM output cannot be verified — these are the system-level answers.

We also cover the broader ML security concerns beyond prompt injection: adversarial inputs designed to fool the model, poisoned training data, and model extraction attacks. These have been demonstrated against deployed systems. They are not theoretical.

By the end, you have built an AI product end-to-end. A prediction engine with a conversational front-end. <span class="accent">The integration is straightforward. Making it safe enough to trust is the real work.</span>
