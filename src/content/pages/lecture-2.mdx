When you train one model, you remember what you did. When you train fifty, you don't. This lecture introduces experiment tracking — the discipline that makes it possible to reproduce, audit, and improve your work over time.

But tracking is only half the problem. The other half is deciding which model goes to production. In a notebook, you pick the one with the highest accuracy. In production, accuracy is one quality among many: a model that is 3% less accurate but 10× faster and costs a tenth to run may be the right choice. Two models with identical accuracy can have different fairness profiles across user segments. The right model for production fits the constraints — and those constraints come from the business, not the notebook.

Students track experiments, compare models across multiple dimensions, and write a justification for which model to ship. Going from "I built a model" to "I shipped the right model for these constraints" is the real shift.
